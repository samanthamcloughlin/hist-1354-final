How does colonization impact linguistic evolution of Creole languages?

And how can linguistic distance reflect and complicate how we think about colonial influence?

Ndyuka is an English-based Creole spoken by Maroon people throughout Africa, centered on Suriname. Suriname was a Dutch colony until 1975, when it gained independence. Source.

Tok Pisin is an English-based Creole spoken in the Solomon Islands, which were colonized by Britain, Germany, and the Dutch East Indies Company. It was largely developed by labourers forcibly brought from the Islands to Queensland plantations, where the common language was predominately simplified English. It's now the lingua franca of Papua New Guinea. Source. Source.

Seychellois is a French-based Creole spoken in the Seychelles. The Seychelles was under French control from 1756 until 1810, when they surrendered control to the British - who controlled the island until 1976. Source. Source.

Naija is an English-based Creole spoken in Nigeria. After interactions with Portuguese and Dutch traders, Nigeria fell under British colonial rule in the 1700s. Nigeria gained its independence in 1960. Source.

Louisiana Creole is a French-based Creole spoken in Louisiana (United States). It's a heavily endangered language, with less than ten thousand speakers remaining. Louisiana was a French colony (and briefly a Spanish colony) from 1682 until the Louisiana purchase in 1803. Source.

Mauritian Creole is a French-based Creole spoken in the Republic of Mauritius. It's closely related to Seychellois. The Republic of Mauritius was briefly controlled then abandoned by the Dutch, and then brought under control of the French from 1709 to 1812. In 1812, the British assumed control of the country, which they maintained until the Republic gained independence in 1968. Source.

Kabuverdianu is a Portuguese-based Creole spoken in Cape Verde. Cape Verde was under Portuguese colonial control as a trading port from the 15th century until 1975. Source.

Saint Lucian is a French-based Creole spoken in Saint Lucia. Starting in the 17th century, the French and British both made attempts to colonize Saint Lucia, with the French finding more success. In 1814, Saint Lucia was ceded by the French to Britain. Saint Lucia gained independence in 1979. Source. Source.

Trinidadian Creole is an English-based Creole spoken in the Republic of Trinidad and Tobago. While initially colonized by the Spanish, Spanish control was weak and few ensalved people were brought over. In fact, the dominant influence at that time was French, due to the influx of migrants from the French West Indies. In 1797, Britain gained control of the Republic, and brought over a huge influx of enslaved people from other British colonies. In 1962, the Republic gained its independence. Source.

Papiamento is a Portuguese-and-Spanish-based Creole spoken in Curaçao. Curaçao was colonized by the Spanish in 1515, who enslaved and deported the indigenous popuplation. The Dutch gained control in 1634, and Curaçao remains today in the Kingdom of the Netherlands. The country saw many Portuguese and Spanish speaking migrants - for example, Sephardic Jews immigrating from Portugal - which influenced its linguistic development. Source.Source.

Guadeloupean is a French-based Creole spoken in Guadeloupe. Guadeloupe was colonized by the French in 1635, and is still an "overseas department" of France today. Source.

Haitian is a French-based Creole spoken in Haiti. Haiti was colonized by the French from 1697 to 1804, when it gained independence after the Haitian Revolution. Source. Source.

Perplexity-Based Linguistic Distance

What is linguistic distance?

Linguistic distance is a measure of how different two languages are. There are many proposed measures of linguistic difference, such as ease of second language learning, or comparing synonyms.

What is perplexity?

Perplexity "measures the confidence model has in its (next word) predictions." It proxies how well a model "understands" a text.

What is perplexity-based linguistic distance?

Pichel et al define perplexity-based linguistic distance "by comparing the n-grams of a text in one language or period of language with the n-gram model trained for the other language. This comparison must be made in the two directions."

To formalize it: PLD(L1, L2) = (PP(CHL2, LML1) + PP(CHL1, LML2))/2

Some Creole Examples

These examples use corpuses assembled by JHU researchers, no pre-trained models.

Haitian:English: 16885

Ndyuka is an English-based Creole - for example, 76% of its most common 200 words are sourced from English. Interestingly enough, Suriname and French Guiana (the primary location of Ndyuka speakers) were not dominated by the British in the colonial period. While there were some British claims to the area, the primary colonizer was the Netherlands (speaking Dutch). On the other hand, Haitian is a French-based Creole, and Haiti was a french colony for over a century. Thus, the comparatively large linguistic distance makes sense.

Haitian:French: 15778

Haitian:English: 16885

Now, comparing English to French, we can see French is comparatively closer to Haitian. Notable, however, is that the difference is much smaller than expected. While part of this most definitely speaks to the sample size, part of it also speaks to how evolved Haitian has become. Haitian Creole is estimated to be the most popular Creole language, and has grown far past mutual intelligibility with French (including its own grammar).

Insights

Broad takeaways - A first notable insight is English's outsized impact even on regions where it had a relatively smaller colonial presence. This could be a reflection of a few things. First, the popularity English has gained likely impacts the evolution of Creole languages, especially the retention of words in the post-colonial era. Given English is a way more common second language than Dutch, for example, it makes sense that its impact has more longevity.
Second, compared to empires like the Dutch that were mostly focused on trading hubs, the English colonial strategy of settlement likely also contributes to its linguistic impact. Accordingly, it's misleading to solely think of linguistic evolution in terms of colonial rule - instead, the broader context of trade networks, settlement, and global linguistic evolution should be considered.
A great example of the misleadingness of focusing only on colonial rule is Louisiana. Louisiana was a French colony, so its Creole is French-based. However, given its purchase by the United States and current status as an American state, it's unsurprising that English has made a huge mark on its language. Another similar example is Seychellois. Despite being a French based Creole, the impact of English is pronounced due to over a century of British rule.

Haitian - In some ways, the work confirms what we suspect about Haitian Creole - eg that the impact of French would be larger than the impact of English. But in other ways, the work reveals how comparatively far Haitian Creole has developed away from both French and English - especially compared to other Creoles that remain closer. The comparative distance of Haitian Creole points to its robust development as its own language, and the strength and independence of Haitian cultural development beyond colonial influence.

Ndyuka - Ndyuka also complicates our expectations around the results. Despite being a Dutch colony, Ndyuka's Creole has incredibly strong ties to English. Interestingly enough, there is very little publicly accessible information documenting why this is the case. This work points to a gap in our current understanding of Ndyuka's evolution. Better understanding how English came to have an out-sized impact on the Ndyukan language could also give us insight into the cultural interactions of the Ndyukan people in Suriname and French Guiana. And, the existing gap in information points to how understudied many of these Creole languages are, despite their potential to be incredibly informative about the history of their people.

Limitations

Sample limitations - Despite both millions of Creole speakers and the centrality of Creole languages, language modelling work with Creole languages often suffers from lack of data and attention in the field. For this project, the biggest sample size issues came when computing the proper PLD, which is why it is only computed for 3 language pairs (which each had millions of words in its corpus, comparable to other projects ). There's also issues surrounding data quality - for example, all text is converted to unicode (stripping accents and unique characters) which obscures connections based on non-unicode characters. Future work that enlargens the corpus would be a significant improvement.

Processing power - When calculating the perplexities with gpt2, our data was also limited by processing power. In order to prevent my computer from breaking, the test sample for each language is limited to 1000 entries (despite the availability of more data).

Legitimacy of Perplexity-Based Linguistic Distance - While there have been mulitple published papers using perplexity-based linguistic distance, it's a relatively new concept and far from a definitively accurate way to calculate linguistic distance. While it has the benefit of accounting for the concept of mutual intelligibility in a manner that more structured methods cannot, it also risks missing many important linguistic aspects: for example, over-estimating superficial similarities and under-estimating the importance of grammatical structure.
